📐 Sentence Structure

Hindi word order: Subject → Object → Postposition → Verb → To-be verb
English uses prepositions BEFORE nouns. Hindi uses postpositions AFTER nouns.
Pihu Bharat se hai. (Pihu is FROM India — "se" comes AFTER "Bharat")
Wah class mein hai. (He is IN class — "mein" comes AFTER "class")

Questions

Question words slot in where the answer would go.
Kaun piano baja raha hai? — "Kaun" replaces the person's name.
Both forms acceptable: Piano kaun baja raha hai? or Kaun piano baja raha hai?

🔄 Apna / Apne Aap — "One's Own" / "By Oneself"

Apna/Apni/Apne = one's own. Use when the subject refers to their OWN possession — NOT someone else's.
The key question: who owns it, and are they the subject of the sentence?
HindiEnglishWhy?
Woh apne papa ke saath rehti thiShe used to live with her own dadShe = subject, she owns the dad relationship
Woh uske papa ke saath rehti thiShe used to live with his/someone else's dadSomeone else's dad
Woh apna kaam karta haiHe does his own workHe = subject, his own work
Main apne ghar ja raha hoonI am going to my own homeI = subject, my own home

Apne aap — "By oneself / By yourself"

Woh apne aap karta hai. — He does it by himself.
Apne aap kar lo. — Do it yourself.
Woh apne aap theek ho jayega. — It will get better on its own.
Main apne aap khush hoon. — I am happy with myself.

🆕 Modal Verb Structures (from your notes!)

5. Paana — "Managed to / Was able to" (completed result)

Use root verb + paya/payi/paye. Implies you actually succeeded (stronger than sakna).
Main kar paya. — I managed to do it. (m)
Woh ja nahi payi. — She couldn't go / didn't manage to go. (f)
Hum mil paye. — We managed to meet.

6. Dena — "Let / Allow"

Use oblique infinitive (-ne form) + do/deta. Change -na → -ne before dena.
Mujhe jaane do. — Let me go. (jaana → jaane)
Woh karne deta hai. — He lets (someone) do (it).
Mujhe bolne do. — Let me speak. (bolna → bolne)

7. Lena — "Do for yourself"

Use root verb + lo. Implies doing something for your own benefit.
Kha lo. — Eat (for yourself). | Le lo. — Take (for yourself).
Samajh lo. — Understand (for yourself / get it into your head).

8. Wala/Wali/Wale — "About to / Going to"

Use infinitive + wala/wali/wale + hoon/hai/hain. Expresses something is just about to happen.
Woh aane wala hai. — He is about to come. (m)
Barish aane wali hai. — Rain is about to come. (f)
Hum jaane wale hain. — We are about to go.

The "-ne" form (oblique infinitive)

Used with Dena (let) and Paana (managed to). Simply change -na → -ne at the end of the infinitive.
Infinitive-ne form
jaanajaane
karnakarne
bolnabolne
sonasone
likhnalikhne
Compare the three infinitive forms:
-na (full) → used with Chahna, Sakna: main jaana chahta hoon
root stem → used with Sakna, progressive: main ja sakta hoon
-ne (oblique) → used with Dena, Paana: mujhe jaane do

🎯 Ko / Mujhe — Dative Constructions

Ko marks the indirect object ("to/for someone"). When combined with pronouns it creates special forms. These are essential for expressing feelings, needs and preferences.
Pronoun + koFormMeaning
Main + komujhe / mujhkoto me / I feel
Tum + kotumhe / tumkoto you (familiar)
Aap + koaapkoto you (formal)
Woh + kouse / usseto him/her
Hum + kohamein / hameto us
Ve + kounhe / unkoto them

Mujhe — Expressing feelings & needs

Mujhe bhook lagi hai. — I am hungry. (lit. hunger has attached to me)
Mujhe pasand hai. — I like it. (lit. it is pleasing to me)
Mujhe pata hai. — I know. (lit. knowledge is to me)
Mujhe neend aa rahi hai. — I am feeling sleepy.
Mujhe darr lag raha hai. — I am scared. (lit. fear is attaching to me)
Mujhe chai chahiye. — I need/want tea.
Mujhe abhi jaana hai. — I have to go now.
Mujhe pata nahi. — I don't know.
Key insight: In Hindi, feelings "happen to" you rather than you "having" them. You don't say "I am hungry" — you say "hunger came to me". This is why mujhe is so commonly used!

🔧 Ne Construction — Ergative Past

For completed past actions with transitive verbs (verbs that take an object), the subject takes the "ne" marker and changes form. The verb agrees with the OBJECT not the subject.
Subject+ Ne formExample
MainmaineMaine khaana khaaya. (I ate food.)
TumtumneTumne kya kiya? (What did you do?)
AapaapneAapne achha kiya. (You did well.)
WohusneUsne kitaab padhi. (He/she read the book.)
HumhumneHumne kaam kiya. (We did the work.)
VeunhoneUnhone khaana banaya. (They cooked food.)
⚠️ Intransitive verbs DON'T use ne: Jaana, aana, sona, uthna, bhaagna — these never take ne in past tense.
✅ Woh aaya. (He came.) — NO ne because jaana/aana is intransitive.
✅ Usne khaana khaaya. (He ate food.) — ne because khaana is transitive.

✅ Chahiye — Need / Should

Chahiye expresses need or "should". It's invariable — it doesn't change for gender or number. The person who needs uses the dative form (mujhe/tumhe/aapko etc.)
Mujhe chai chahiye. — I need/want tea.
Aapko aaram chahiye. — You need rest.
Kya chahiye? — What do you need?
Aapko jaana chahiye. — You should go. (infinitive + chahiye)
Hume milna chahiye. — We should meet.
Use doctor ke paas jaana chahiye. — She should go to the doctor.
With noun: mujhe [thing] chahiye = I need [thing]
With infinitive: mujhe [jaana] chahiye = I should [go]

⚡ Obligation — "Have To" (Jaana + Hai/Tha)

To express obligation ("have to / must"), use: dative pronoun + infinitive + hai/tha
This is different from chahiye — it expresses stronger obligation/necessity.
Mujhe jaana hai. — I have to go.
Mujhe jaana tha. — I had to go.
Tumhe kaam karna hai. — You have to work.
Use school jaana hai. — He/she has to go to school.
Hamein milna hai. — We have to meet.
TenseFormExample
Present (have to)infinitive + haiMujhe jaana hai
Past (had to)infinitive + tha/thi/theMujhe jaana tha
Future (will have to)infinitive + hoga/hogi/hongeMujhe jaana hoga

🔀 Conditionals — Agar...To (If...Then)

Agar = if | To = then (often optional in speech)
Word order: Agar + [condition], to + [result]
Agar tum aao, to main bhi aaunga. — If you come, I will also come.
Agar baarish ho, to hum nahi jayenge. — If it rains, we won't go.
Agar tumhe bhook hai, to kuch khao. — If you're hungry, eat something.
Agar woh phone kare, to mujhe batao. — If she calls, tell me.
Agar mere paas waqt hota, to main aata. — If I had time, I would come.
Agar mujhe pata hota, to main tumhe batata. — If I knew, I would tell you.
Warna = otherwise (alternative to agar): Jaldi jao, warna late ho jaoge. (Go quickly, otherwise you'll be late.)

⚡ Emphatic Particles — Hi & Bhi

Hi — Only / Exactly / Emphasis

Hi follows the word it emphasises and can't easily be translated — it adds stress/exclusivity.
Main hi karunga. — Only I will do it. (emphasises Main)
Abhi hi jao. — Go right now. (emphasises abhi)
Yahi hai. — This is exactly it. (yeh + hi)
Wahi tha. — It was exactly that. (woh + hi)
Tab hi aana. — Come at that exact time.

Bhi — Also / Even / Too

Bhi follows the word it applies to — it adds inclusion.
Main bhi aaunga. — I will also come.
Woh bhi nahi aaya. — He didn't come either.
Yeh bhi theek hai. — This is also fine.
Kuch bhi. — Anything at all.
Kabhi bhi. — Anytime at all.

📝 "Pehle" — Alternative "Used To"

Two ways to say "used to" in Hindi:
1. Grammar form: root + ta/ti/te + tha/thi/the
2. Word-based: pehle + root + ta/ti/te + hai (present tense!)
They used to eat meat → Woh maas khate the (grammar form)
They used to eat meat → Woh pehle maas khate the (with emphasis — "they PREVIOUSLY ate meat")
He previously eats → Woh pehle khaata hai (current habit framed as "previously he does this")
The pehle form adds emphasis — it stresses the contrast between then and now. Your tutor Prakash uses both — both are correct.

⏰ All Tenses at a Glance

1. Present Habitual — "Usually does"

root + ta/ti/te + hai/hain/hoon
Main roz chai peeta hoon. (I drink tea every day.)
Woh kaam karti hai. (She works.)

2. Past Habitual — "Used to"

root + ta/ti/te + tha/thi/the
Main Bharat mein rehta tha. (I used to live in India.)
Woh tennis khelta tha. (He used to play tennis.)

3. Present Progressive — "Is doing"

root + raha/rahi/rahe + hai/hain/hoon
Main TV dekh raha hoon. (I am watching TV.)
Woh so rahi hai. (She is sleeping.)

4. Past Progressive — "Was doing"

root + raha/rahi/rahe + tha/thi/the
Wahan kya ho raha tha? (What was happening there?)
Woh khaana bana rahi thi. (She was cooking food.)

5. Sakna — "Can / Is able to"

root + sakta/sakti/sakte + hai/hain/hoon
Main Hindi bol sakta hoon. (I can speak Hindi.)
Woh aa sakti hai. (She can come.)
Negative: Main nahi bol sakta. (I cannot speak.)

6. Chahna — "Want to"

infinitive + chahta/chahti/chahte + hoon/hai/hain
Main India jaana chahta hoon. (I want to go to India.)
Woh piano bajaana chahti hai. (She wants to play piano.)
With noun: Main chai chahta hoon. (I want tea.)

🔤 Sakna vs Chahna — Key Difference

Sakna (Can)Chahna (Want)
Verb formROOT verb stemFULL infinitive (-na)
ExampleMain bol sakta hoonMain bolna chahta hoon
NegativeMain nahi bol saktaMain nahi chahta
Standalone❌ Cannot stand alone✅ Main chahta hoon (I want)
Pehle / Ab structure (Past vs Present contrast)
Pehle main cricket khelta tha, ab nahin khel sakta.
(I used to play cricket, now I cannot play.)

🏗️ Negative Sentences

Add nahin/nahi before the main verb (or before sakta/chahta).
Woh school nahi jaati. (She doesn't go to school.)
Main meat nahi kha sakta. (I cannot eat meat.)
Woh aaj nahi aa rahi. (She is not coming today.)

Paana — "Managed to"

root + paya / payi / paye
Main kar paya. (I managed to do it.)
Woh ja nahi payi. (She couldn't go.)
Hum mil paye. (We managed to meet.)

Wala — "About to / Going to"

infinitive + wala/wali/wale + hai/hain/hoon
Woh aane wala hai. (He is about to come.)
Barish aane wali hai. (Rain is about to come.)
Hum jaane wale hain. (We are about to go.)

🎯 Ko / Mujhe Constructions — "To me / For me"

Ko — Direction / Need / Feeling

Person + ko + [noun/feeling/need]
Mujhe bhookh lagi hai. (I am hungry.)
Usse pyaas lagi hai. (He/she is thirsty.)
Mujhe nahi pata. (I don't know.)

Chahiye — "Need / Should have"

Person + ko + noun/infinitive + chahiye
Mujhe chai chahiye. (I need/want tea.)
Usse doctor ke paas jaana chahiye. (He should go to the doctor.)
Humko khaana chahiye. (We need food.)

Accha Lagna — "Like / Feel good"

Person + ko + [thing] + accha lagta/lagti hai
Mujhe chai acchi lagti hai. (I like tea.)
Usse Bollywood movies acche lagte hain. (He likes Bollywood movies.)
Mujhe yeh gana accha lagta hai. (I like this song.)

Ko Pronoun Table

Direct → Ko form
Main → Mujhe | Hum → Hume/Humko
Aap → Aapko | Tum → Tumhe
Woh → Use/Usse | Ve → Unhe/Unko

🔄 Ergative Construction — Ne (Completed Past)

When to use Ne

Subject + ne + object + verb (past)
Maine khaana khaaya. (I ate food.)
Usne letter likha. (He/she wrote a letter.)
Humne kaam kiya. (We did the work.)

Ne Pronoun Changes

Main → Maine | Hum → Humne
Maine → I (completed action)
Tumne → You | Aapne → You (formal)
Usne → He/She | Unhone → They

Verb agrees with OBJECT (not subject)

No -ta/-ti — use kiya/ki/kiye etc.
Maine chai pi. (I drank tea — chai=f → pi)
Usne gana gaaya. (He sang a song — gana=m → gaaya)
Humne movies dekhi. (We watched films — f → dekhi)

Ne NOT used with

Motion/intransitive verbs — no Ne!
Main gaya. (I went — NOT Maine gaya)
Woh aayi. (She came — NOT Usne aayi)
Hum soe. (We slept — NOT Humne soe)

❓ Conditionals — Agar...To (If...Then)

Real Conditional (likely)

Agar [present/future], to [future/present]
Agar kal barish hogi, to main nahi aaunga. (If it rains tomorrow, I won't come.)
Agar aap padenge, to aap pass ho jayenge. (If you study, you will pass.)

Habitual Conditional

Jab bhi [habitual], tab [habitual]
Jab bhi main Bharat jaata hoon, main dosa khaata hoon. (Whenever I go to India, I eat dosa.)
Jab tum aaoge, tab milenge. (When you come, we'll meet.)

Key Conditional Words

agar = if | to = then | warna = otherwise
Agar tum nahi aaye, to woh chali jayegi. (If you don't come, she will leave.)
Jaldi aao, warna late ho jaoge. (Come quickly, otherwise you'll be late.)

📢 Commands & Imperatives

Tum (informal) — Root verb

ROOT (no ending)
Ja! (Go!) | Aa! (Come!) | Kha! (Eat!)
Bol! (Speak!) | Dekh! (Look!) | Sun! (Listen!)

Aap (formal) — Root + iye/iye

Root + iye/ijiye
Jaiye/Jaiye! (Please go!) | Aaiye! (Please come!)
Boliye! (Please speak!) | Dekhiye! (Please look!)
Baitiye! (Please sit!) | Lijiye! (Please take!)

Negative Commands — Mat

Mat + root verb (or Mat + infinitive)
Mat ja! (Don't go!) | Mat bol! (Don't speak!)
Mat karo. (Don't do it.) | Mat khao. (Don't eat.)
Chinta mat karo. (Don't worry.)

Soft Requests — Zara / Please

Zara + command = softer request
Zara ruko. (Wait a moment.)
Zara suniye. (Please listen a moment.)
Kripya + command = very formal please

⚖️ Comparatives & Superlatives

Comparative — "More than" (Se)

[A] [B] se zyada/bada/accha hai
Woh mujhse zyada khaata hai. (He eats more than me.)
Delhi Mumbai se bada hai. (Delhi is bigger than Mumbai.)
Yeh usse accha hai. (This is better than that.)

Superlative — "Most / Best" (Sabse)

Sabse + adjective
Woh sabse accha hai. (He is the best.)
Yeh sabse mehnga hotel hai. (This is the most expensive hotel.)
Main sabse chota hoon. (I am the youngest.)

Key Comparison Words

Adjective pairs
bada/chota — big/small | accha/bura — good/bad
zyada/kam — more/less | lamba/chota — tall/short
purana/naya — old/new | sasta/mehnga — cheap/expensive

Equally as... (Jitna...Utna)

Jitna [A], utna [B]
Jitna kaam, utna paisa. (More work, more money.)
Woh utna hi accha hai jitna uska bhai. (He is just as good as his brother.)

📋 Obligation — "Have to / Must"

Weak Obligation — Chahiye

Infinitive + chahiye (should)
Aapko samay par aana chahiye. (You should come on time.)
Usse Hindi seekhni chahiye. (She should learn Hindi.)

Strong Obligation — Padna / Parega

Mujhe/Usse + infinitive + padega/padegi/padenge (have to)
Mujhe jaana padega. (I will have to go.)
Usse kaam karna padega. (He/she will have to work.)
Humko yahan rehna padega. (We will have to stay here.)

Must / Compulsory — Zaroori Hai

Infinitive + zaroori hai (it's necessary)
Yahan rehna zaroori hai. (It is necessary to stay here.)
Usse doctor ke paas jaana zaroori hai. (It's essential he sees a doctor.)

⏮️ Perfect Tenses — "Has done / Had done"

Present Perfect — "Has done"

Subject + verb (past form) + hai/hain/hoon
Main Bharat gaya hoon. (I have been to India.)
Usne khaana khaaya hai. (He/she has eaten.)
Kya aap kabhi London gaye hain? (Have you ever been to London?)

Past Perfect — "Had done"

Subject + verb (past form) + tha/thi/the
Main ja chuka tha. (I had already gone.)
Woh khaana kha chuki thi. (She had already eaten.)
Hum mil chuke the. (We had already met.)

Chukna — "Already completed"

root + chuka/chuki/chuke + hai/tha
Woh ja chuka hai. (He has already gone.)
Main khaana kha chuka hoon. (I have already eaten.)
Hum so chuke hain. (We have already slept.)
